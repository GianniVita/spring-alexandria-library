Hidden in a small Italian village stands the “Alexander Library” — not a royal archive, but a personal kingdom of stories, ideas, recipes, dreams, and memories.
Built slowly through the years by me and my mother, every shelf holds a piece of our lives: books about cooking, travel, psychology, languages, cinema, family, and growth. Some are old and worn, others forgotten treasures waiting to be opened again.

The library is part imagination, part reality — a place where curiosity lives quietly between the pages. A small village library with the spirit of something much bigger.

Of course, it is not the legendary Library of Alexandria in Egypt, where thousands of ancient scrolls once carried the knowledge of civilizations. Yet, in its own humble way, this little library carries something just as human: memory.

Not far from here, there are old schools and agricultural institutes where generations once studied the land, the seasons, animals, dialects, and the rhythm of rural life. There were teachers who may not have been famous scholars, but they knew the territory deeply. They knew the names of the winds, the stories behind the hills, the character of people, and the silent wisdom of everyday life.
This library is also dedicated to them. To the noble writers and poets, yes — but also to the farmers, workers, mothers, and humble people who never wrote for glory, yet still left behind traces of deep feeling and truth. Some of them confronted poverty, isolation, and the limitations of their small world, but they still observed life with honesty and humanity.

Today we learn from modern books, digital archives, and endless information. But sometimes the greatest treasures are hidden in simple pages written by ordinary people — pages filled with emotion, dialect, memories, and lived experience.
The “Alexander Library” is not about perfection or prestige. It is a quiet collection of human voices, where ancient wisdom, rural knowledge, family memories, and modern curiosity all rest together on the same shelves.
